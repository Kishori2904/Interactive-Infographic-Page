Apollo 11 Mission

The First Human Landing on the Moon

Mission Overview

Apollo 11 was the spaceflight that first landed humans on the Moon. Commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin formed the American crew that landed the Apollo Lunar Module Eagle on July 20, 1969. Armstrong became the first person to step onto the lunar surface six hours and 39 minutes later; Aldrin joined him 19 minutes later.

They spent about two and a quarter hours together outside the spacecraft, and collected 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of lunar material to bring back to Earth. Command module pilot Michael Collins flew the Command Module Columbia alone in lunar orbit while they were on the Moon's surface.

Legacy

Apollo 11 effectively ended the Space Race and fulfilled a national goal proposed by President John F. Kennedy in 1961: "before this decade is out, of landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth."

The mission laid the groundwork for all future lunar exploration missions and represents one of humanity's greatest technological achievements. The landing site at the Sea of Tranquility is now a historical landmark and protected site.
